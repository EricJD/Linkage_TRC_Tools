Chapter 20 u00a7u00a7 1001-1011, established judicial definitions and coastal prohibitions for the United States maritime industry. The Act invoked the accords of the International Convention for the Prevention of the Pollution of the Sea by Oil, 1954. The international agreement provided provisions to control the discharge of [[fossil fuel]] [[Pollutants#Pollutants|pollutants]] from [[Chemical tanker|nautical vessels]] on the [[International waters|high seas]].The S. 2187 [[legislation]] was passed by the United States 87th Congressional session and enacted by the [[Timeline of the presidency of John F. Kennedy|35th President of the United States]] John F. Kennedy on August 30, 1961.==History==The [[International Convention for the Prevention of Pollution of the Sea by Oil]] (OILPOL) was an [[Public international law|international convention]] organized by the [[United Kingdom]] in 1954. The convention was held in [[London|London, England]] from April 26, 1954, to May 12, 1954. The international meeting was convened to acknowledge the disposal of harmful waste which posed endangerment to the [[marine ecosystem]]s.{{cite web |url=http://www.admiraltylawguide.com/conven/oilpol1954.html |title=International Convention for the Prevention of Pollution of the Sea by Oil, 1954 |date=May 12, 1954 |publisher=Admiralty and Maritime Law Guide |accessdate=November 25, 2013}}The International Convention for the Prevention of the Pollution of the Sea by Oil, 1954 original text was penned in [[English language|English]] and [[French language|French]]. The 1954 international agreement was amended in 1962, 1969, and 1971.{{cite web |url=http://www.admiraltylawguide.com/conven/oilpolamend1962.html |title=Amendments to the International Convention for
